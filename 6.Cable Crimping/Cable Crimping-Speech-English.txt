 "Module 5: Cable Crimping and Labeling. > The details of crimping and labeling are what separate high-quality installation from substandard work. A loose crimp or an incorrect, obscured label compromises system reliability, creates intermittent faults, and adds unnecessary hours to future troubleshooting. We must ensure a standard of excellence—treating every single termination as a critical connection that requires precision. > Let's review our field mandates step-by-step, as illustrated in the layout guidelines. > **One. Drawing Reference and Wiring Preparation.** Always refer strictly to your wiring schematic before preparing your materials. Pre-route the cable along its designated pathway right up to the terminal block *before* making the cut. Always ensure there is a comfortable, relaxed slack loop. A wire must never be under tension or pulled tight after it is terminated. > **Two. Strict 4S Cleanliness.** Professionalism means keeping a pristine workspace. Leaving insulation strips, cut copper bits, or plastic cable tie clippings on the floor, inside the control panel, or at the bottom of any junction box is strictly prohibited. Keep a designated waste box directly at your workstation and place all scrap debris into it immediately. > **Three. Perfect Stripping and Zero Damage.** Set your wire strippers to the exact insulation cut length required by the lug barrel. Slice the insulation cleanly. If your tool nicks, shaves, or cuts even a single inner copper strand, stop, cut the wire back, and strip it again. When inserting the core, ensure there are absolutely zero loose or splayed strands sticking out backward from the lug collar. > **Four. Select the Right Lug Type and Exact Die.** Match the lug to the terminal connection specified in the engineering drawing. Use Pin Ferrules for push-in terminal blocks and PLC modules, Ring Lugs for heavy-duty power studs and earth bars, and Fork Lugs for standard screw-clamp barrier strips. Most importantly, verify that your crimping tool and jaw die size match the wire cross-section perfectly. > **Five. Correct Full-Cycle Crimping.** Execute your crimps with proper technique. Grip the mechanical ratchet tool correctly and compress it through its entire cycle until the jaws fully close on the lug barrel and automatically release. Immediately follow it up with a firm, physical two-finger manual pull-test to verify that the mechanical bond is one hundred percent secure. > **Six. Correct Labeling Direction and Visibility.** Slide your printed identification sleeve ferrules onto the wire *before* inserting it into the terminal. Align the label so the printed text faces directly outward and reads cleanly from left-to-right or bottom-to-top. Anyone opening this panel must be able to read every code effortlessly at a single glance without having to twist or strain the wire. > **Seven. Systematic Routing and Neat Terminal Termination.** Ensure the wires emerge uniformly from the plastic wire ducts, forming neat, parallel loops as they enter the terminal block. Avoid crisscrossing or tangled overlaps at the terminal jaw. Align them squarely, insert the lug cleanly, and tighten firmly to ensure a reliable electrical connection. > **Eight. Stop and Report Error.** If a crimp feels loose, if a sleeve splits, if a label code is smudged and unreadable, or if the physical terminal block destination does not match your layout schematic, stop work immediately. Do not attempt a temporary fix, and do not guess. Stop work and inform your supervisor right away. > By focusing on these precise details, we ensure that the inside of every panel is organized, safe, and engineered to the highest standards."
