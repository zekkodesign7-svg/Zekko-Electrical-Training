Cable Handling and Routing Mandates. Let’s be completely honest with ourselves: a sloppy cable job is the fastest way to invite hidden system failures, signal drops, and unexpected maintenance nightmares. Cable routing isn't just about getting a wire from point A to point B; it’s about treating the cable like the critical lifeline of the entire automation system. We need a permanent mindset shift—from just 'running wires' to executing pristine engineering. Look at the six mandates on your screen in image, and let's break down how we work out in the field. One. Minimum Bending Radius. Treat your cables with respect. When moving around corners, always maintain a wide, smooth bending radius. Forcing a cable into a sharp, tight 90-degree kink destroys the internal insulation and physically fractures the copper strands inside. If it looks crimped or flattened, it’s wrong. Two. Parallel Laying and Dressing. Open up a tray and it should look like a work of art. Cables must be laid completely straight, flat, and perfectly parallel to each other. Get rid of the chaotic, tangled mess. No overlapping, no twisting, and no crisscrossing. Neatly dressed cables keep the system cool and make future troubleshooting effortless. Three. Edge Drag Protection. Never, under any circumstances, drag a raw cable directly over sharp metal tray edges, concrete floors, or rough steel structures. The sharp steel will slice or tear the outer protective sheath instantly. Use cable rollers and guide pulleys at transition points to shield the cable during the pull. Four. Power and Signal Segregation. We must fight electrical noise. Keep your high-voltage power lines and your sensitive control or communication signal wires completely separated. Run them in separate compartments partitioned by a solid metal divider plate. Mixing them together ruins signal integrity. Five. Uniform Tying and Support. When cables climb a vertical ladder tray up a pillar, they need structural support. Secure them at exact, uniform regular intervals using neat ties. But remember—do not over-tighten your zip ties. If you pinch or crush the outer cable jacket, you are damaging the core. Keep it firm, neat, and evenly spaced. Six. Damage and Kink Reporting. Mistakes happen, and cables get caught. But the biggest mistake you can make is trying to hide it. If you discover a severe kink, a deep scratch, or a crushed section while unrolling a drum, stop pulling immediately. Do not cover it up with electrical tape. Speak up, point it out, and report the damage to your supervisor right away. Finally, look at other image to see what true professionalism looks like. Notice how the heavy black cables are bundled neatly, descending straight down the side of the panel structure with smooth, gentle curves leading into the heavy industrial plug connectors. Every single entry point is perfectly aligned, securely supported by the frame, and free of stress or tension. Let's match this exact level of clean execution on our floor. Take pride in your routing, protect the cable material, and always build it to last."
